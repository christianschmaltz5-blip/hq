1 · Site analysis — the 30–32 unit thesis breaks first

Density is measured per acre, not per building — the neighbor's "16 units per building" doesn't transfer. At the adjacent parcel's 29 du/ac base (Phoenix R-4A), 0.826 acres yields 24 units by right, and only after a rezone: the parcel's current institutional use is almost certainly not zoned multifamily today. Reaching 30–32 units means 36–39 du/ac — R-5 or a PUD with bonuses, a longer and riskier entitlement (12–18 months, ~$150–250K of soft cost before permits either way).

Physically, 32 units does not fit. Parking at Phoenix's ~1.5–1.7 spaces/unit blended (~50 spaces ≈ 17,000 SF with aisles) + a two-story 32-unit footprint (~12,000 SF) + setbacks, required open space, and Phoenix's on-site stormwater retention (basin eats 8–12% of an infill lot) ≈ 38,000 SF of demand on a 36,000 SF site. Three stories barely squeezes in, deep into entitlement-stretch territory.

Realistic program: 24–26 units, garden walk-up, surface parked.

4–5 · Value, spread, and returns — where it dies

Phoenix Q1 2026 cap rates: Class A ~4.74%, Class B ~4.92%, all-class average ~5.6% (Northmarq / Kidder Mathews). New boutique product in a C+ corridor takes a small-asset liquidity discount — exit at 5.25–5.5%.

  • Stabilized value: $4.8–5.0M vs. all-in cost of $6.5M at ask → development spread ≈ −$1.5M to −$1.7M (−23%).
  • Yield on cost: 4.1% vs. a 5.25% market cap — building to negative leverage. Merchant developers need ~6%+ YOC.
  • 32-unit stretch case: ~$7.9M all-in, $340K NOI, 4.3% YOC, ~$6.5M value → still ~$1.4M underwater.
  • IRR / equity multiple: profit is negative before promote, so returns are negative at any leverage.

The structural problem: $1,400–1,500 rents cannot carry ~$230K/door construction. This site needs roughly $1,950–2,000/month to reach a 6% yield on cost — ~35% above today's GCU-corridor market.

6 · Residual land value

Working backward from a 6% required yield on cost: supportable total cost = $264K ÷ 0.06 ≈ $4.4M, versus $5.5M of cost before land. Residual land value ≈ −$1.1M. The maximum a rational apartment developer can pay for this dirt today is zero — and they still lose. No honest underwriter outbids you on the development thesis.

The real story on price: the seller isn't pricing land. $999K ≈ $250/SF for a 2014 all-block building — at or below replacement cost for that construction — with 36,000 SF of parking-rich land attached. Their market is another owner-user (school, daycare, congregation), and against that buyer pool $999K isn't crazy, which is why $750K was rejected. You're not competing against developers here; you're competing against a church with a building fund.

Recommendation: walk away from it as a development site. As an income deal, re-engage at ≤$800–850K contingent on a signed or highly probable NNN tenant at $14+/SF — and treat the 29 du/ac neighbor as free long-term option value you never pay for today. The only world where ground-up returns here is West Camelback rents at ~$2,000/month; don't pay 2026 prices for a 2030 thesis.
